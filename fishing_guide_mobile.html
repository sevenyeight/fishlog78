Fishing Guide

Gear, casting, lures, reading water — everything you need to start catching fish with confidence.

Your core setup (spin fishing)

You're on a telescopic pole + spinning reel setup — solid beginner combo. Here's how the whole system works together.

1
Rod — Your telescopic rod works fine. A 6–7 ft medium-light to medium rod is ideal. Longer rod = more casting distance.
2
Reel — The Zebco 33 Spincast is the right call. Closed-face design physically prevents bird's nests — just press and hold a thumb button to cast.
3
Line — Use 8–12 lb monofilament. Leave 1/8 inch from the spool rim. Overfilling = tangles.
4
Snap swivel — Tie one at the end of your line. Swap lures in seconds AND prevents line twist from spinning lures like Rooster Tails.

The bird's nest problem — solved

CauseFix
Line overfillFill spool to 1/8" from rim only

Overhead cast — the essential cast

Used 90% of the time. With a Spincast reel, press and hold the thumb button throughout.

1
Hold — Grip the handle. Press and hold the thumb button on the back of the reel.
2
Position — Point rod toward target at 2 o'clock. Lure should hang ~12 inches from rod tip.
3
Load — Sweep rod back smoothly to 10–11 o'clock. Feel it flex slightly under the lure weight.
4
Release — Sweep forward, release button at 1–2 o'clock when pointing at your target. Timing controls accuracy.
5
Follow through — Let rod tip follow toward target. Feather the button to slow lure before splash.

Sidearm cast — low overhangs

Same mechanics, rotated 90° sideways. Use when trees or bridges block overhead space.

  • Cast parallel to the water surface
  • Great for skipping lures under docks — one of the best fish-holding spots
  • Lower power — use for closer targets

Accuracy tips

Release timing is everything. Too early = cast goes high and short. Too late = hits right in front of you. Practice aiming at a bucket in your backyard before your next trip.
  • Pick a specific target — a rock, a shadow on the water. Don't cast "out there" blindly
  • Power comes from the rod tip, not your arm — smooth flick beats a hard throw
  • Cast into the wind for better accuracy
  • 10–15 mins of backyard practice before a trip pays off fast

Your starter lure kit

These cover most freshwater situations in Southern California.

Best beginner

Kastmaster (⅜ oz)

Heavy spoon that casts far. Cast out, let it sink a few seconds, reel at medium speed. The wobbling flash mimics an injured baitfish. Colors: chrome, gold, blue/chrome.

Trout & bass

Rooster Tail (¼ oz)

Spinning blade creates flash and vibration. Slow-to-medium retrieve — blade must spin. Use a snap swivel. Colors: white/red, black, yellow.

Step 2 lure

Zoom Fluke

Soft plastic jerkbait. Rig weightless on a 3/0 hook. Cast near bank, twitch-twitch-pause. Deadly for bass in shallow cover.

All-rounder

Mepps #2 Spinner

Classic inline spinner. Great for trout, bass, and crappie. Keep the blade spinning on the retrieve.

The retrieve mindset

Most beginners reel back to shore as fast as possible just to cast again. This is the #1 mistake. The lure is only fishing when it's in the water moving correctly.

Slow down. Most beginners retrieve 2–3x too fast. Slower gives fish time to react. Experiment until you find what works that day.

The count-down method

How to figure out what depth fish are holding at — essential for consistent catches.

1
Cast out. Let lure sink, counting "1, 2, 3..." as it falls.
2
Start retrieve at "3" (shallow) first. No bites? Try at "6", then "10".
3
Once you get a bite at a specific count, repeat that count every cast. You've found the depth.

Cover the water (fan casting)

  • Fan casting — Cast at 10, 11, 12, 1, 2 o'clock. Cover 180° before moving
  • Work the edges — Cast parallel to the bank, close to structure. Fish sit on the EDGE of cover
  • Move 10–15 feet down the bank after covering an area with no bites

Detecting & setting the hook

  • Keep rod tip up during retrieve — maintains tension so you feel bites
  • Bites feel like: a thump, sudden weight, slack line, or just "different" resistance
  • When you feel something — set the hook immediately. Quick upward wrist snap. Don't hesitate.
  • Keep steady pressure while reeling — slack = hook falls out

Where fish actually are

Fish follow three things: food, cover, and comfort. Find these and you find fish.

Structure & cover to target

Docks & piers

Bass love the shade. Cast parallel to the dock, skip lures under it. Posts = ambush points.

Fallen trees / brush

Cast right to the edge. Getting occasionally snagged means you're fishing close enough.

Rocky banks & points

Rocks hold heat, attract baitfish. Fish the edges where rock meets soft bottom.

Weed lines

Cast to the EDGE of weed lines, not into them. The edge concentrates fish.

Inlets & outlets

Moving water = oxygenation and food. Fish often stack here, especially summer.

Drop-offs

Where shallow meets deep — one of the most productive areas in any lake.

When you hook a fish

1
Stay calm, keep tension. Rod tip up, reel steadily. If the fish runs, let it — but maintain pressure.
2
Pump and reel. Lower rod, reel up. Raise rod, reel again. Repeat until fish is close.
3
Land it. Wet your hand first. Lip-grab bass by the lower jaw. For trout, support the body with both hands.
4
Remove the hook using needle-nose pliers. Back it out the same direction it went in.

Keeping fish (catch-to-table)

1
Dispatch humanely. Firm rap on the skull right behind the eyes with a small club. Fast and prevents suffering.
2
Bleed (optional but improves taste). Cut the gills, place in water for 30 seconds. Dramatically improves the meat flavor.
3
Ice immediately. Pack directly on ice. Fish quality degrades fast in heat.
4
Clean within a few hours. Scale, gut, and rinse. Watch a 5-min YouTube tutorial for your species.

Catch & release

  • Minimize air time — keep fish in water as much as possible
  • Wet your hands before handling — dry hands strip their protective slime coat
  • If deeply hooked, cut the line — the hook will dissolve naturally
  • Revive by holding underwater, pointing into current, until it swims away on its own

Essential knots (learn in this order)

1
Improved Clinch Knot — The most important knot. Ties line to hook, swivel, or lure. Takes 2 minutes to learn. Do this first.
2
Palomar Knot — Stronger than clinch for braided line. Simple and very secure.
3
Loop Knot (Rapala Knot) — Free-swinging loop for better lure action. Noticeably improves smaller lures.

Resources that accelerate skills fast

  • YouTube: "Tactical Bassin" and "1Rod1ReelFishing" — great for shore fishing beginners
  • App: Fishbrain — shows where local anglers are catching fish, with species and lure details
  • Talk to people at the lake. "What are you using today?" — most anglers are happy to share
  • Fish the same spot repeatedly. Familiarity with one lake beats fishing five different ones

The honest truth

Everyone at the water is focused on their own fishing — nobody is watching or judging you. The people who look "expert" were beginners not that long ago. The fastest way to stop feeling like a novice is to go more often. Each trip builds pattern recognition and muscle memory that compounds. You're already doing the right things.
