Rod Selection
Foundation of your setup
🥇
Medium Spinning Rod — 6'6" to 7'
Best beginner pick
Versatile for lures and bait. Easy to cast, forgiving of mistakes. Works in rivers, lakes, and surf. Look for "medium power, fast action" on the label.
📡
Telescopic Rod
What you currently have
Totally fine to use. Slightly less sensitive than a one-piece rod but perfect for learning. Make sure all sections are fully locked before casting.
Pro Tip
The rod's "action" matters more than brand. Fast action bends near the tip — better lure control. Moderate action bends in the middle — more forgiving for beginners.
🔄
Reel Selection
Most important upgrade for you
🥇
Zebco 33 Spincast
Top pick — eliminates bird's nests
Push-button cast, enclosed spool. Completely solves your tangling problem. Under $30. Perfect for metal lures. Available at Walmart and Big 5.
🥈
Open-Face Spinning Reel (2500–3000)
Next step up when ready
More versatile long-term. Shimano Sienna or Ugly Stik combo ~$40–60. Requires learning proper bail control — the main cause of bird's nests.
Bird's Nest Cause
Bird's nests happen when line releases before the rod loads on a cast, or the bail closes too early. A Spincast reel physically prevents this with its enclosed mechanism.
🧵
Line
The connection between you and the fish
🥇
Monofilament — 8–12 lb test
Best for beginners
Stretchy, forgiving, easy to knot. 10 lb mono is perfect for most California freshwater fishing. Fill reel to 1/8" below the spool lip — not to the top.
🔄
Braided Line — 15–20 lb (with leader)
Intermediate upgrade
No stretch, extremely sensitive. You'll feel every pebble. Attach a 2–3 ft mono leader so fish can't see it. Lasts much longer than mono.

Color Selection
Simple rule to follow
☀️
Clear water, sunny day
Natural, realistic colors — silver, chrome, translucent. Fish see well and reject anything fake-looking.
🌊
Murky or stained water
Bright and loud — chartreuse, orange, gold. Fish hunt by vibration and need to spot your lure visually.
☁️
Overcast or low light
Gold and copper work great — they flash even in dim conditions. Also try white or pearl.

The universal fallback. Every fish eats them. Thread onto a #8 hook leaving a tail. Works best with a split shot weight.
🫧

Floating dough bait loaded with scent. Let it sit 6–18" off the bottom. Stocked trout absolutely crush it.
🦐

For surf/pier fishing. Shrimp works for bass, perch, and corbina. Anchovies attract everything in the surf.
Casting

Master these and you'll look like you've fished for years.

The Overhead Cast
Start here
  1. Hold rod at 2–3 o'clock, lure hanging 12–18" below tip
  2. Press and hold thumb button (spincast) or open bail and pinch line with index finger
  3. Bring rod back to ~10 o'clock — no further, or you lose power
  4. Swing forward toward your target — accelerate through the motion
  5. Release button (or line) when rod reaches 10 o'clock on the forward swing
  6. Follow through, letting rod tip point at where you want to land
Most Common Mistake
Releasing too early (lure goes up) or too late (lure hits right in front of you). Practice over grass before fishing to nail the timing.
Sidearm Cast
Learn second
Same motion as overhead but parallel to the ground. Use when there are trees behind you or a low bridge overhead. More accurate for close targets.
Pendulum / Lob Cast
Learn second
Let the lure swing like a pendulum and lob it forward. Ideal for tight spots, docks, and when you need precise placement with minimal backswing.
Pitch Cast
Intermediate
Hold the lure in your free hand, pull slight tension in the line. Swing rod tip forward while releasing. Very quiet, pinpoint entry. Used to drop lures under docks where bass hide.
🔄
Retrieve Styles
How you reel in matters as much as the cast
1
Steady retrieve
Reel at a constant pace. The standard starting point. Faster = shallower. Slower = deeper.
2
Stop-and-go
Reel a few turns, pause, reel again. The pause often triggers a strike — predators react to prey that suddenly stops.
3
Twitch retrieve
Flick the rod tip while reeling to give erratic action. Makes your lure look like a wounded, panicked baitfish.
4
Countdown method
Count to 5 before retrieving, then 10, then 15. Keep going deeper until you find where fish are holding.
Golden Rule
If no bites after 20 minutes, change your retrieve speed first. Fish are picky about how fast their food is moving.
🐟
Fighting & Landing Fish
What to do when you get a hit
1
Set the hook
Sweep the rod tip up firmly — don't yank violently. With circle hooks, just reel; they set themselves.
2
Keep the rod bent
Maintain constant pressure. A bent rod acts as a shock absorber. Slack line = lost fish.
3
Use the drag
If the fish runs hard — let it pull line off. Don't lock down on a running fish or the line snaps. Reel when it pauses.
4
Land it
Reel the fish close, reach under its belly or lip. Use a net if you have one — much easier.
Essential Knots

Learn these 3. They handle 95% of all situations.

Improved Clinch Knot
Use for: Attaching lure or hook to line

The most common fishing knot in the world. Learn this one first.

  1. Thread 6 inches of line through the lure eye
  2. Wrap the tag end around the main line 5–6 times
  3. Pass tag end back through the loop right next to the eye
  4. Pass it through the big loop you just created
  5. Wet the knot with saliva, pull main line tight
  6. Trim the tag end close
Critical
Always wet the knot before tightening. Dry knots create friction heat that weakens the line by up to 30%.
Palomar Knot
Use for: Lures, hooks — especially with braid

Stronger than the clinch. Preferred knot for braided line.

  1. Double 6 inches of line and thread the loop through the lure eye
  2. Tie a simple overhand knot with the doubled line (don't tighten)
  3. Pass the loop over the entire lure or hook
  4. Pull both ends to tighten, sliding loop to eye
  5. Wet and tighten firmly, trim tag
Surgeon's Knot
Use for: Joining main line to leader

When you use braided line, attach your mono leader with this.

  1. Overlap 6 inches of both lines, running parallel
  2. Make a loop with both lines together
  3. Pass both lines through the loop twice
  4. Wet the knot, pull all 4 ends simultaneously to tighten
  5. Trim both tag ends
Knot Strength Test
Before fishing, always pull your knot hard with your hands. A knot that slips under hand pressure will fail when a fish hits. Re-tie if there's any doubt.

Reading the Water
Where fish actually hide
🌿
Vegetation & Weed Edges
Cast to the edge of lily pads or underwater grass — not into the middle. Fish ambush prey from cover.
🪵
Fallen Trees & Structure
Sunken logs and docks are fish city. Bass love dark, shaded cover. Cast tight to the structure, retrieve parallel to it.
🌊
Current Breaks
In rivers, fish hold behind rocks and bends where current slows. They wait for food to drift by without burning energy.
📉
Drop-offs & Depth Changes
Where shallow drops to deep is a highway for fish. Use the countdown method to find the shelf depth.
🌀
Points & Coves
Land points concentrate fish during feeding. Sheltered coves warm faster in spring, drawing early bass.
Timing & Conditions

Fish feed on a schedule. Being there at the right time matters more than technique.

🕐
Best Times of Day
Ranked by bite activity
Dawn
⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Golden Hour
30 min before sunrise to 2 hours after. Water is cool, light is low, fish feed aggressively near the surface. When the big ones come out.
Morning
⭐⭐⭐⭐ Still Good
2–4 hours after sunrise. Activity slows but fish are still catchable. Move to deeper water as the sun climbs.
Midday
⭐⭐ Slow Period
Fish go deep or into shade to avoid heat. If you fish now, go deep and slow your retrieve significantly.
Afternoon
⭐⭐⭐ Wakes Up
After 3–4pm, activity picks back up. Shadows lengthen, water cools. Fish start feeding ahead of evening.
Dusk
⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Second Golden Hour
Equal to dawn — sometimes better for bass. Last hour of light + 30 min after sunset. Fish aggressively along structure.
Night
⭐⭐⭐ Catfish Prime Time
Catfish feed most actively at night. Use darker lures, go slow. Check local regs — some areas prohibit night fishing.
☁️
Weather Conditions
Read the sky, catch more fish
⛅
Overcast
Best conditions. Fish roam higher, feeding more aggressively all day long.
🌧️
Before Rain
Barometric pressure drops = feeding frenzy. Go when rain is forecast 4–8 hours out.
🌈
After Rain
Wait a day. Murky water disorients fish. Use bright, loud lures to compensate.
☀️
Sunny & Hot
Dawn and dusk only. Fish go deep and lethargic midday. Work shade and deep drop-offs.
💨
Windy
Wind pushes food to the downwind bank. Cast from upwind toward where baitfish pile up.
🥶
Cold Front
Day after a cold front hits, fishing tanks. Wait 2–3 days for fish to readjust.

Advanced Tactics

Level up from catching nothing to consistently catching fish.

🧠
The Fisherman's Mindset
Think like a predator
1
Move if nothing in 20 minutes
Don't fish an empty spot. No bites in 20 min = change location or presentation. Fish vote with their mouths.
2
Always be changing something
Lure color, retrieve speed, depth, location. Each cast is data. Change one variable at a time until you crack the code for that day.
3
Follow the food
Baitfish rippling on the surface = predators underneath. Cast into or around that activity immediately.
4
Use a journal
Note time, weather, lure, depth, retrieve, and what worked. Patterns emerge over time. This separates consistent anglers from lucky ones.
🎯
Stealth & Presentation
Stop spooking fish before they see your lure
👣
Walk softly near the bank
Fish feel vibrations via their lateral line. Heavy footsteps alert fish 20+ feet away. Approach silently.
👤
Stay low, cast at an angle
Fish see you against the skyline in clear water. Crouch when approaching shallow spots.
☀️
Keep your shadow off the water
A shadow crossing the water is a predator alarm. Position yourself so your shadow falls behind you.
💧
Soft entries win
A splashy landing near a fish sends it fleeing. Aim for a gentle plop, not a crash.
🐟
Species-Specific Tactics
Different fish, different approach
🌈
Rainbow Trout (Stocked)
PowerBait on the bottom in 4–8 feet. Or Rooster Tail retrieved medium-fast. Fish near where they were stocked. Early morning is critical.
🐟
Largemouth Bass
Target structure — docks, logs, vegetation edges. Morning and evening with topwater or spinnerbaits. Midday, go deep with a Ned rig dragged slowly.
😸
Channel Catfish
Bait only — stink bait, chicken liver, or nightcrawlers. Bottom rig, heavy weight. Fish at night near the deepest point. Let them run before setting the hook.
Watching Others
If you see other anglers catching fish, observe without being intrusive. Note their rod, casting spot, and retrieve. Fastest education available.

Catch, Keep & Handle
What to do after you land one
🪝
Hook Removal
Grip the hook shank with pliers. Push the hook back in the direction it went in to back it out. Never pull forward against the barb.
🧊
Keep fish fresh
Dispatch humanely (sharp blow to top of head), then immediately into your cooler on ice.
↩️
Catch & Release
Keep fish in water as much as possible. Wet your hands before handling. Return the fish headfirst into the current and hold until it swims away.
Know Your Limits
California bag limits: usually 5 trout/day. Bass minimum: typically 12". Check current rules at wildlife.ca.gov before you go. Citations are expensive.
🤝
Fishing Etiquette
How not to be "that guy"
📏
Give other anglers at least 50 feet of space. Don't crowd someone who's catching — it's bad form even if tempting.
🚫
Never cast across someone else's line. If you cross by accident, both anglers reel in and untangle calmly.
🗑️
Pack out ALL your trash — especially monofilament line. Wildlife gets tangled in discarded line.
🔇
Keep noise down near the water. Loud conversations don't just annoy people — they spook fish.
🙌
Congratulate people when they catch. The fishing community is friendly — put positive energy out and you'll get tips back.
